# 2048 -- the weekend and the spike (March 2014)

## Who made it

**Gabriele Cirulli.** Italian, 19 years old at the time, working as a
UI designer / web developer. Not a game developer. 2048 was a personal
project, not a product.

## How it was built

Cirulli wrote 2048 in **JavaScript and CSS, over a weekend**, and
published the source on GitHub on **9 March 2014** under the MIT
license. From his own retrospective:

> I built 2048 in a weekend, just for fun. I had become addicted to two
> other games, called 1024! and 2048.

(The "2048" he refers to as inspiration is a slightly earlier minimalist
web version by a developer using the handle Saming; Cirulli's version
united it with the 1024 ruleset and gave it the visual identity that
made it travel.)

He emphasised in the same post:

> The game would remain open-source, and that I wouldn't bring it to
> mobile.

This was a deliberate ethical stance: he did not want to monetise a
game built so directly on someone else's work. He later softened this
and put out a mobile version himself, in part to displace the third-
party clones already on the App Store; the development of that mobile
port took him "a full month from scratch."

## The launch

On **9 March 2014**, Cirulli posted the game on **Hacker News**. Just
the title, "2048," and a link.

It ranked near the top for an extended period and accumulated
approximately **3,000 upvotes** -- unusually high for the HN audience,
which tends toward criticism. The thread is at
[news.ycombinator.com/item?id=7373566](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=7373566).

Cirulli has described the moment of realisation:

> I checked my Google Analytics and saw about a thousand people on the
> page at the same time, and the number just kept growing.

Within roughly a week, the site had taken **over four million unique
visitors**. By the end of the first month, the figure was in the
**tens of millions**; the eventual tally for the original site reached
above **23 million** distinct players, with widely cited (though
unverifiable) man-hour totals well beyond that.

A representative tweet of his from those weeks, quoted in *TechCrunch*:

> 28000 people playing #2048game right now. The amount of man-hours
> spent playing this game will never be returned to humanity.

## Why it spread

Several mechanical factors, none of which are mysterious in retrospect:

- **Zero friction.** A web URL, no install, no payment, no account.
- **A round is short.** Five to twenty minutes; people played at desks.
- **The rules fit a sentence**, and the score is a power of two -- a
  hook for the developer audience that first saw it on HN.
- **The source was MIT-licensed and well-organised.** Forks and
  variants appeared within days; many of them then went viral
  independently, recursively feeding back into the original's traffic.

Coverage moved from Hacker News to Reddit to general tech press
(*Wired*, *The Verge*, the *Wall Street Journal* -- the WSJ description
"almost like *Candy Crush* for math geeks" is widely quoted) within
about a week. By that point the game had stopped being a developer
curiosity and was a mainstream phenomenon comparable, in the press
framing, to *Flappy Bird* the previous month.

## Cirulli's stated feelings at the time

From his Medium post a few weeks later:

> [It was] one of the most exciting [periods of my life ... and] one of
> the most stressful.

He has consistently named *Threes!* in interviews as the game his own
"owes its existence to," and has expressed sympathy with Vollmer and
Wohlwend's frustration -- something it is worth holding alongside the
controversy covered next, because the public story sometimes flattens
him into a villain he does not appear to have been.
