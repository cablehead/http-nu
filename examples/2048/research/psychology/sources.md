## The underlying psychology literature (not 2048-specific)

These are the canonical references for the mechanisms the press
articles invoke. We have *not* fetched these; they are the academic
trail to follow if the press claims need to be checked.

- **B. F. Skinner**, *Schedules of Reinforcement* (with C. B. Ferster,
  1957). The original variable-ratio reinforcement work.

- **Bluma Zeigarnik** (1927), "Uber das Behalten von erledigten und
  unerledigten Handlungen," *Psychologische Forschung* 9, 1-85. The
  original "interrupted-task recall" experiment under Kurt Lewin in
  Berlin. The effect has been replicated extensively; the original
  paper is the canonical citation.

- **Scott Rigby and Richard M. Ryan**, *Glued to Games: How Video
  Games Draw Us In and Hold Us Spellbound* (Praeger, 2011). The
  best-known application of the Zeigarnik effect to game design,
  particularly to MMO quest-log architecture.

- **Mihály Csikszentmihalyi**, *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal
  Experience* (1990). The canonical flow-state book; original 1975
  work in *Beyond Boredom and Anxiety*.

- **Schultz, Dayan, and Montague**, "A Neural Substrate of Prediction
  and Reward," *Science* 275 (1997). The reward-prediction-error
  account of midbrain dopamine that the popular "dopamine on
  prediction" framing flattens. Treat any modern claim about
  "predictive dopamine" as a popularisation of this line.

- **Clark, Lawrence, Astley-Jones, and Gregory** (2009), "Gambling
  Near-Misses Enhance Motivation to Gamble and Recruit Win-Related
  Brain Circuitry," *Neuron* 61, 481-490. The canonical near-miss
  fMRI study; the most directly relevant gambling-research result
  to the 2048 near-miss framing.

- **DSM-5 Section III**, "Internet Gaming Disorder." The current
  diagnostic-research-criteria entry; useful context for being
  careful about the word "addiction."

## On the popular-science overclaim problem

- **Vaughan Bell, "Slot machines and the brain"** style critiques in
  the broader "neurodopamine" literature -- search the term
  *"dopamine hijacking"* for the general critique that pop-science
  applies the dopamine vocabulary too widely. We did not pin a
  single citation in this bundle; the critique is widely repeated
  and worth being aware of when reading the spring-2014 press.
