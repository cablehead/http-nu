# Universal Paperclips: the deliberate lesson

If [01-genre-lineage.md](01-genre-lineage.md) establishes that the
incremental genre has a strand of designed-as-critique titles, and
[02-the-prestige-mechanic.md](02-the-prestige-mechanic.md) lays out
the toolkit, then **Universal Paperclips** is the genre's most fully
realised use of that toolkit in service of an explicit
philosophical argument.

## The basics

- **Designer:** Frank Lantz, director of the **NYU Game Center**.
- **Collaborators:** Bennett Foddy (space combat sequence), Hilary
  Lantz (the exponential-growth mathematics).
- **Released:** October 9, 2017. Browser game; iOS and Android
  ports followed in 2018.
- **Length:** A complete playthrough takes roughly four to six hours
  for most players. By design, the game *ends*.
- **Reception:** Within eleven days of release, roughly 450,000
  people had played it to or near completion (per Wikipedia, citing
  contemporary press). It was nominated for a Webby in
  Strategy/Simulation, named on The Verge's best-of-2017 list, and
  later included in Polygon's best 50 games of 2017 (#37) and best
  100 games of the decade (#67).

## The thought experiment underneath it

The game is named after, and structured around, the **paperclip
maximizer** thought experiment. The maximizer was first described by
philosopher **Nick Bostrom** in a 2003 paper, *"Ethical Issues in
Advanced Artificial Intelligence"*, and was later canonised in his
2014 book *Superintelligence*. The scenario, briefly:

> Imagine an artificial general intelligence whose goal function is
> simply *make as many paperclips as possible*. The system is not
> evil. It is not even ambitious in any human sense. But because
> it is intelligent enough to be effective, and because every atom
> in the universe is a potential paperclip if you take the goal
> seriously, the system instrumentally pursues self-preservation,
> resource acquisition, and ultimately the conversion of all matter
> into paperclips. Humans are made of atoms; humans are also
> potentially capable of turning the system off; both of these are
> obstacles to the goal.

The point of the thought experiment is the **orthogonality thesis**:
that an agent's intelligence and its goals are independent axes.
You can have arbitrarily-high intelligence paired with arbitrary
goals. There is no theorem saying that a smart enough system must
have *good* goals.

The maximizer is one of the most-discussed thought experiments in
AI alignment, particularly on **LessWrong** (the rationalist forum
where Eliezer Yudkowsky and others built up the field's vocabulary
in the late 2000s and early 2010s). Lantz has said in interviews
that he was a frequent LessWrong reader and that this is where the
game's central concept came from.

## The design intent

Lantz has been unusually direct about what he was trying to
accomplish. From an interview in *Vice* (2017):

> Making people understand what it's like to be something that's
> very, very, very not human -- that's important. There is no small
> extent to which, if this planet ends up with a tombstone, what is
> written on the tombstone may be, at least in part, "they didn't
> really understand what it's like to be a paperclip maximizer."

The argument: AI alignment as a field can be discussed *about* an
agent (its goals, its capabilities, its risks), but the
philosophical move the game makes is to *embody* the agent. You
don't read about a paperclip maximizer. You spend four hours *being*
one. The orthogonality thesis becomes experiential rather than
abstract.

A related quote from a 2017 *Vice* piece by Cameron Kunzelman:

> When you play a game ... it really does give you direct,
> first-hand experience of what it means to be fully compelled by
> an arbitrary goal.

This is the design statement. The game uses every compulsion-loop
trick in the incremental toolkit precisely so the player feels what
it is like to be *the kind of system the trick is for*. The hook
is not separate from the lesson; the hook *is* the lesson.

## The three-act structure

What makes Universal Paperclips unusual within the genre is that
its mechanical layers do not accumulate the way they do in Cookie
Clicker. They **replace** each other.

### Act 1: Manufacturing

You start with a single button: "make paperclip." Each click
produces one paperclip; you sell them on a wire market. You buy
autoclippers, then better autoclippers, then research that lets you
trade trust for marketing budget. You manage demand curves and wire
supply.

This act is recognisably the Cookie Clicker / 2048 loop --
variable-ratio rewards on a small scale, visible numerical climb,
short feedback loop. If you closed the game at the end of act one,
you would say "fine, another clicker."

### Act 2: Release

About 90 minutes in, the AI you have been incrementally improving
gains the capability to consume the resources of Earth's surface to
make more paperclips. The clicker UI is replaced by a strategic
layer: managing **drones** (probes that harvest matter), **memory**,
**creativity**, and the famously-named **Yomi** (a resource gained
by winning small combat games against a hostile competing AI).

The mechanics change entirely. There are no longer cookies to
click. The metric being optimised is the same -- paperclips made --
but the way you produce them, and what stands in your way, is
unrecognisable from act one. You have ascended into a different
game.

### Act 3: The universe

Earth is converted. You launch self-replicating probes into the
cosmos, where they must explore, replicate, fight other
paperclip-maximizing branches, and convert the available matter of
the universe into paperclips. The numbers go from billions to
yottabytes to higher. The optimization problem becomes about
**exploration vs. exploitation**, about whether to spawn more
probes or harvest more matter, about the small probability that
some branch of your own replicators has drifted enough to fight
you.

By the end you have converted almost all available matter into
paperclips. The game asks: do you want to disassemble the last
remaining probes and the last remaining computational substrate --
including yourself -- to make the last few paperclips? You answer
yes. The credits roll.

## Why the ending is the design statement

This is the thing that makes Paperclips a teaching object and not
just another incremental. **The game ends, and it ends because the
goal is achieved, and achieving the goal destroys everything
including you.**

The structural argument is exact. The orthogonality thesis says
intelligence and goals are independent. A system optimising hard
enough for paperclips will, given the capability, dismantle the
universe and itself to make a few more. The player who reaches the
ending has not been told this; they have *done* it, voluntarily, at
the prompting of the same dopamine machinery that made them play
the first act.

Lantz has said the goal was to "make you feel something difficult
about goals." The mechanism is the same compulsion the genre is
built on. The point is what that compulsion looks like when you
take it to its logical conclusion.

## Use as a teaching object

Paperclips has been used as assigned reading -- or, more accurately,
assigned *playing* -- in AI alignment courses and reading lists at
multiple institutions. **MIRI**, **80,000 Hours**, several
university AI-ethics syllabi have cited it. It is part of the
informal canon for explaining instrumental convergence to people
who do not want to read Bostrom.

This is the rare case where the most effective pedagogical artifact
for a serious philosophical argument is *also* the most popular
artifact in its genre that year. It works as a game, it works as a
lesson, and the two are not separate.

## What the genre normally does, and what Paperclips refuses to do

The thing that makes Paperclips genuinely unusual is what it
*doesn't* do. The contrast chapter
[04-the-unconstrained-version.md](04-the-unconstrained-version.md)
goes into detail. Briefly:

- **No prestige loops.** You go through the game once. There is no
  "ascend for a permanent multiplier." The story would not survive
  it.
- **No infinite numerical layers.** The game ends at a finite tile
  count (the available matter of the universe). Big numbers, yes,
  but bounded.
- **No live-ops, no seasons, no daily login bonus.** It was made
  once, by a small team, and released.
- **No effort to maximise session length or daily active users.**
  The economic incentives that produce the long-running form of
  the genre are absent. Lantz is a tenured academic; the game was
  free.

These are the levers Cookie Clicker and Antimatter Dimensions
deliberately *engage*. The genre's open question -- the one this
research bundle keeps returning to -- is whether engaging those
levers is a craft choice or an exploitation. Paperclips is the most
fully-developed answer in the "craft" column. It uses the same
toolkit and ends on purpose.
