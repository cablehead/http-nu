# Sources

## Primary statements by designers

- **Frank Lantz, "Universal Paperclips"** -- the game itself.
  <https://www.decisionproblem.com/paperclips/>

- **Frank Lantz's faculty page at NYU Game Center** -- biographical
  background, design statements.
  <http://www.franklantz.net/>

- **Ian Bogost, "The Curse of Cow Clicker"** -- Bogost's own blog
  post on his experience with Cow Clicker as a satire that
  succeeded too well.
  <http://bogost.com/writing/blog/the_curse_of_cow_clicker/>

- **Cow Clicker, Bogost project page** -- official documentation,
  links to the GDC Online talk and other primary statements.
  <https://bogost.com/games/cow_clicker/>

- **Cow Clicker (live).**
  <https://www.cowclicker.com/>

- **Cookie Clicker (official).**
  <https://orteil.dashnet.org/cookieclicker/>

- **Orteil's tumblr** -- the developer's primary blog. Includes
  "The cult of the cookie clicker: When is a game not a game?", a
  primary statement from Orteil on the genre's status as a game.
  <https://orteil42.tumblr.com/>

## Press and coverage

- **Jason Tanz, *Wired*, "The Curse of Cow Clicker: How a Cheeky
  Satire Became a Videogame Hit"** (January 2012 issue). The
  canonical write-up of Cow Clicker's success-against-intent
  trajectory.
  Republished excerpts:
  <https://uminnpressblog.com/2011/12/21/wired-the-curse-of-cow-clicker-how-a-cheeky-satire-became-a-videogame-hit/>

- **NPR, "Cow Clicker Founder: If You Can't Ruin It, Destroy It"**
  (November 2011) -- on the cowpocalypse.
  <https://www.npr.org/2011/11/18/142518949/cow-clicker-founder-if-you-cant-ruin-it-destroy-it>

- **Game Developer (formerly Gamasutra), "GDC Online: Ian Bogost's
  Troubling Experiences With Cow Clicker"** -- coverage of Bogost's
  GDC Online talk.
  <https://www.gamedeveloper.com/game-platforms/gdc-online-ian-bogost-s-troubling-experiences-with-i-cow-clicker-i->

- **Cameron Kunzelman, *Vice*, "This Game About Making Paper Clips
  Has Cured Me of Twitter"** (2017) -- the press piece that most
  directly captures Lantz's stated design intent.
  <https://www.vice.com/en/article/paperclips-game-clicker-frank-lantz/>

- **Aaron A. Reed, "1F50: 2017 -- Universal Paperclips"** -- a
  long-form retrospective from his Interactive Fiction history
  series, useful for context on the game's design and reception.
  <https://if50.substack.com/p/2017-universal-paperclips>

- **Vice, "'Cookie Clicker' Wasn't Meant to Be Fun. Why Is It So
  Popular 8 Years Later?"** -- includes the Orteil Polygon-quote
  about "accumulating large amounts of things."
  <https://www.vice.com/en/article/cookie-clicker-wasnt-meant-to-be-fun-why-is-it-so-popular-8-years-later/>

- **Inverse, "Prestige 'Cold War': How Call of Duty fixed a broken
  'D&D' mechanic"** -- the most-cited contemporary piece on the
  prestige-mechanic lineage. Includes the BatMUD (1990) and NetStorm
  (1997) claims. Treat the early-history claims as load-bearing on
  this single source.
  <https://www.inverse.com/gaming/prestige-cold-war-call-of-duty>

## Academic background (cited but not fetched)

- **Nick Bostrom**, "Ethical Issues in Advanced Artificial
  Intelligence" (2003). The first published version of the
  paperclip maximizer.

- **Nick Bostrom**, *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies*
  (Oxford University Press, 2014). The canonical book on
  instrumental convergence and the orthogonality thesis.

- **LessWrong**, the rationalist forum where AI-safety vocabulary
  (including the paperclip maximizer as a popular reference) was
  developed in the late 2000s and early 2010s. Lantz has cited it
  as a direct influence.
  <https://www.lesswrong.com/>
