# Genre lineage: a tradition of self-critique

The incremental genre is unusual in that several of its foundational
titles were built explicitly as **satire** of compulsive game design.
The satires went viral. The serious-design successors that came
after them are, in important ways, descendants of jokes that landed
too hard.

This is not a complete history -- the *Incremental game* Wikipedia
entry has the full taxonomy. This chapter picks out the titles that
matter for understanding why the genre has the shape it does.

## Progress Quest (Eric Fredricksen, 2002)

The proto-incremental. A satire of EverQuest and the late-1990s MMO
grind. You create a character; the game then plays itself. Your
character wanders, kills monsters, levels up, finds loot. You watch.
There is no input mechanism after character creation.

The point, deliberately made: if the loop of "small reward, small
reward, small reward" is sufficient to keep you engaged, then the
*player* is structurally optional. The game proves the case by
removing the player and seeing whether the reward stream is still
compelling. For some non-trivial number of people, it was.

Progress Quest is not a clicker -- there is nothing to click -- but
it established the genre's central provocation: that the
reinforcement schedule, not the agency, is the load-bearing part.

## Cow Clicker (Ian Bogost, July 2010)

Cow Clicker is the most explicit satire-becomes-the-thing story in
the genre. **Ian Bogost** is a game-studies professor (Georgia Tech
at the time, now Washington University) and a critic of social-game
design practices, particularly Zynga's FarmVille. He built Cow
Clicker on Facebook to make a point: click a cow, get one point,
wait six hours, click again. Pay real money to skip the timer or
buy a different cow.

Bogost has been on record about the intent: *"I didn't set out to
make it fun ... Players were supposed to recognize that clicking a
cow is a ridiculous thing to want to do."*

What happened next is documented in **Jason Tanz's** *Wired* feature
"The Curse of Cow Clicker: How a Cheeky Satire Became a Videogame
Hit" (January 2012 issue). The game grew from 5,000 to 20,000 to
50,000 players in months. Many of those players were not in on the
joke. Bogost gave a GDC Online talk on the experience and eventually
**"cowpocalypsed"** the game -- a deliberate in-fiction destruction
that ended it.

Cow Clicker matters because it is the first time a designer in this
lineage built the compulsion loop deliberately to indict it, watched
it work, and was disturbed enough to destroy his own game. The
question of whether you can satirise this design pattern without
practising it -- and whether the audience can tell the difference --
is one Bogost was forced to answer in public.

## Candy Box! (aniwey, April 2013)

A French teenager (using the handle aniwey) released Candy Box! as
a browser game. It opens as an ASCII clicker -- you accumulate
candies -- but the game gradually reveals deeper layers: a shop, an
inventory, then a full text-adventure-with-combat hidden inside
what looked like a number-go-up game.

Candy Box! is the first incremental to use the *layered reveal*
structure that Paperclips would later perfect: the game you start
playing is not the game you end up playing. It hides a
non-incremental game inside an incremental wrapper, and the
incremental loop turns out to be the on-ramp, not the destination.

## Cookie Clicker (Orteil / Julien Thiennot, August 2013)

Written in a single evening, posted to 4chan, 50,000 players within
hours. **Julien Thiennot** (handle: Orteil), a French developer,
has said he did not initially intend social commentary -- his
quoted line, from a Polygon interview, is that *"I think what draws
people in is simply the idea of accumulating large amounts of
things over time -- cookies, gold coins, experience."*

But the *game itself* is laced with parody: the upgrades and
achievements have dark-comedy names ("Global Warming"), the
late-game features the **Grandmapocalypse** (grandmothers literally
take over the world), the underlying theme is satirising the
**FarmVille**-era grind. As multiple critics have noted, the genre
absorbed this parody as part of its identity even when individual
authors did not intend it.

The original 2013 Cookie Clicker did not have prestige. **Ascension**
and **Heavenly Chips** were added in version 1.035 in 2014, after
the genre absorbed the prestige loop from elsewhere. The full story
of that mechanic is in
[02-the-prestige-mechanic.md](02-the-prestige-mechanic.md).

## A Dark Room (Doublespeak Games, June 2013)

The third pillar of 2013. A Dark Room starts as a minimal text
interface -- "the room is cold" -- and slowly opens into a survival
game, a village simulation, an exploration roguelike, and finally a
story with a real ending. Like Candy Box!, it hides authored
content under an incremental veneer.

A Dark Room is important because it demonstrates that the genre can
support **authored narrative** -- the very thing that Cookie Clicker
and the post-Cookie-Clicker mainstream have mostly abandoned. The
divergence between A Dark Room's design (which ends) and Cookie
Clicker's (which never does) is the same divergence we will see
later between Universal Paperclips and Antimatter Dimensions.

## What this lineage establishes

By the end of 2013 the toolkit was set: the incremental loop, the
layered reveal, satirical theming, browser distribution, free
access. Two design philosophies were already visible within the
same genre:

- **The Bogost / Candy Box! / A Dark Room strand** -- the genre as a
  vehicle for a *finite* experience, the incremental loop as an
  on-ramp, the design ending on purpose.
- **The Cookie Clicker strand** -- the genre as an *open-ended*
  loop, the satire (where present) as flavor rather than structural
  argument, the game running indefinitely.

Universal Paperclips, four years later, would be the most ambitious
statement of the first strand. Cookie Clicker and the long-running
incrementals that followed -- Antimatter Dimensions, Synergism,
Realm Grinder -- would be the institutional embodiment of the
second.
