# Is "addictive" a compliment or an indictment?

A short tangent off [../psychology/](../psychology/) and
[../incrementals/](../incrementals/). The psychology bundle
describes *how* games hold attention. The incrementals bundle
describes *what designers have built* with that knowledge. This
section is about the **argument inside the field** over whether
building such things is craft or harm.

The argument is real, named, and ongoing. It has well-documented
poles in figures like **Jonathan Blow** (who has called social
games "evil" on the record) and **Frank Lantz** (who treats the
same machinery as morally legible, usable in both directions). It
runs through industry conferences, books, and post-mortems. It is
not settled. It will not be settled.

This bundle has one chapter:

1. [01-the-debate.md](01-the-debate.md) -- the two poles, the
   *Threes!* example as a case where a designer chose against the
   addictive path on purpose, and the economic context that makes
   the argument sharper in some corners of the industry than in
   others.
2. [sources.md](sources.md)

The point of writing this down at all is that the rest of the
research bundle keeps brushing up against the question -- 2048 is
"addictive," Threes! deliberately wasn't, Paperclips uses the
machinery in service of a point -- and treating it as a single,
named, contested topic is more honest than letting it drift through
the other chapters as a vibe.
